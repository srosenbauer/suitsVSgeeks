The Geek Gap: Why Business and Technology Professionals Don't Understand Each Other and Why They Need Each Other To Survive.

What is this Gap?

Suits verse Geeks, many may ask what that even means? Suits are your typical business professionals that help run a business and usually consist of your marketing department's, operations, content managers, sales managers etc. What about Geeks? These are your technology professionals that usually consist of software engineers, UX/UI designers, and system administrators etc.

So what’s the big deal? What about people who seemingly work between this group like product managers? These professionals work directly with the business professionals as well as the technical department of a company? Perhaps, these professionals can offer a solution to this gap?

I conclude this gap exists for two reasons. One, these two groups stereotype each other. Suits see Geeks as people who don’t understand that to run a successful business one has to make money. They think Geeks only view development as a creative art. Geeks on the other hand predominantly view Suits as egocentric profit driven individuals that only see the bottom line and view Geek’s skills as a means to an end a.k.a profit. However, after explaining this to both sides, it was evident that they were confused that this was the case. They both agreed that better communication would be a key to future success. Secondly, it was quite evident that business professionals were quite used to the status quo. They were unable to see the advantages of learning to talk like a “techie”. Although, I don’t believe everyone should know how to code, I believe business professionals in today’s world need to understand the world has changed. The quintessential perfect business professional in the future will understand the basics of programming and how it helps him/her do his/her job more efficiently. Hopefully, through this the communicative gap will be minimized even further.

So, what does this all exactly mean? Is it really one side's fault more than the other? My hope is that through this website you can start to individually learn more about this gap, think critically, and ask yourself how you can help solve a huge business problem in today’s world!

Interviews

As a part of understanding this gap, I thought going straight to the center of the problem, the people, would be the productive way to learn about it. Below are parts of surveys that I conducted this semester. I tried to survey a mix of business professionals, IT professionals, and those that I felt might be in between IT and business.

Anna Muth

Product Manager @ G2Crowd

Question: What’s your job title?
Answer: Product Manager
Question: How long have you worked at your current job?
Answer: 8 months
Question: Would you identify more as a “Geek” or “Suit”?
Answer: Somewhere in the middle!
Question: Do you think “Geeks” and “Suits” have trouble understanding each other in the professional world? If yes, how?
Answer: Yes, it happens frequently. For example, a lot of times when a technology person is talking to a business person, the technology person will use a lot of technical terms and words that the other doesn’t understand. Or they will use a lot of extra technical terms that are not necessary for someone else to understand them.
Question: If you can think of one example in your career where a “Suit” and “Geek” had trouble communicating professionally, please explain the example.
Answer: (paraphrased) Suit Asked: if it’s indicated that a person shares an IP address with other users, are you guys able to tell who the other users are? Geek Responded: Here is the result of the correct query: ```"id","concat","current_login_ip","name" 11786,"DannyRoberts","62.219.35.215","62.219.35.215" 20815,"JoshWeixelbaum","62.219.35.215","62.219.35.215" etc. This was obviously very confusing to the "suit".

Zac Rosenbauer

Software Engineer @ 55 Places

Question: What’s your job title?
Answer: Front-End Software Engineer at 55 Places in Chicago
Question: How long have you worked at your current job?
Answer: almost 7 months
Question: Would you identify more as a “Geek” or “Suit”?
Answer: Geek
Question: Do you think “Geeks” and “Suits” have trouble understanding each other in the professional world? If yes, how?
Answer: Yes, all the time. I encountered this when I started my own business several years ago and until this day I still feel there is a communicative gap. Trying to get the business side of our team to understand what we can and cannot do and ensure that we are building the product they envisioned is always a continuous struggle.
Question: If you can think of one example in your career where a “Suit” and “Geek” had trouble communicating professionally, please explain the example.
Answer:The example that comes to mind is back when I had my own company. There was a product we were working on. I was working closely with one of "suits" and the deadlines the "suit" was setting for these projects were extremely unrealistic. I felt that he didn't understand how much time and work it actually takes to build out a product.

Mark Riemesch

Sales Manager @ Krendl Machines

Question: What’s your job title?
Answer: Sales Manager
Question: How long have you worked at your current job?
Answer: 5 years
Question: Would you identify more as a “Geek” or “Suit”?
Answer: Really neither, but I guess if I had to it would be Suit(I never wear one)
Question: Do you think “Geeks” and “Suits” have trouble understanding each other in the professional world? If yes, how?
Answer: Yes, I don’t work closely with programmers on a regular basis, but the few instances I have worked with a more technically oriented person it has been a challenge. Understanding how they "talk" can be a strain on our ability to complete projects in a timely manner.
Question: If you can think of one example in your career where a “Suit” and “Geek” had trouble communicating professionally, please explain the example.
Answer:No great examples come to mind, but I will say the couple times I have worked closely with IT professionals there has definitely been what I would call a "language" barrier. How I look at a problem and discuss it was definitely different than how they did.
